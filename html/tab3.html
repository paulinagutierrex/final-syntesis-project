General Relativity

What is it?


General relativity is a theory of the gravitational field and general reference systems, published by Albert Einstein in 1915 and 1916. What was it about? The theory of general relativity proposes that the very geometry of space-time is affected by the presence of matter, resulting in a relativistic theory of the gravitational field. In fact, the theory of general relativity predicts that space-time will not be flat in the presence of matter and that the curvature of space-time will be perceived as a gravitational field. Einstein's basic insight was to postulate that at any certain point one cannot experimentally distinguish between a uniformly accelerated body and a uniform gravitational field. The general theory of relativity also made it possible to reformulate the field of cosmology.

Gravity

At the beginning of the 20th century, Albert Einstein developed his general theory of relativity, in which he described gravity as a deformation of space caused by the presence of massive objects, similar to what happens when a heavy ball deforms a rubber strip.
This deformation "indicates" the smaller objects as they should move through space, so that they enter into orbit or fall on the larger astronomical object.

This gave rise to a very different way of visualizing space. In the past, it was believed that space contained a fluid known as "ether", but when no one was able to prove the existence of this substance, humanity began to think that space was simply empty.

Therefore, Einstein's idea that space was like a fabric structure stretched across the universe was revolutionary. He called it the "space-time continuum

«Gravity, whose presence was everywhere a body existed, was not a force in itself, but geometry, the presence of a body in space deformed “space-time” and it was this deformation that attracted the bodies between them»

Space-time

Space and time had their independent identities, and nobody ever got them mixed up. It was with the theory of relativity, put together in the early 20th century, that talking about space-time became almost unavoidable. In relativity, it’s no longer true that space and time have separate, objective meanings. What really exists is space-time, and slicing it up into space and time is merely a useful human convention.

The Newtonian conception of the universe considers that space and time are absolute, which means that they are the same for all those observed regardless of their relative motion. Einstein concludes that measurements of space and time in relativity depend especially on the relative motion of those observed. Moreover, it turned out that space and time are, in fact, intertwined

Space-time-curvature

Einstein's equations tell us how space-time is deformed based on how matter and energy are distributed over it. In the absence of matter and energy, space-time is flat, it has no curvature. This is the so-called Minkowski space-time or special relativity. When there is matter/energy, space-time is curved, with the highest curvature being in those regions where the accumulation of matter/energy is greatest. The most characteristic extreme case is that of a black hole, where the matter/energy density and curvature can become infinite in some regions.
If we know the geometry associated with a certain distribution of matter/energy, Einstein's equations allow us to determine very precisely how small changes in that distribution modify the geometry of space-time. These small changes are known as "disturbances". In the Solar System, for example, the Sun is the dominant source of matter/energy and is primarily responsible for the curvature of space.

Wormhole

In physics, a wormhole, also known as an Einstein-Rosen bridge, is a hypothetical structure associated with a space-time whose topology is multiply connected,seek to explain how one could travel in space-time

This proposal is a theoretical explanation of space-time travel that cannot yet be verified. It has not been possible to know about any with existing space observation technology so far. On the other hand, science still does not have the necessary elements to create a wormhole and experiment with it.

Blackhole

A black hole is a place in space where gravity pulls so much that even light can not get out. The gravity is so strong because matter has been squeezed into a tiny space. This can happen when a star is dying. Because no light can get out, people can't see black holes. They are invisible. Space telescopes with special tools can help find black holes. The special tools can see how stars that are very close to black holes act differently than other stars.

There are four types of black holes: stellar, intermediate, supermassive, and miniature. The most commonly known way a black hole forms is by stellar death. As stars reach the ends of their lives, most will inflate, lose mass, and then cool to form white dwarfs.
